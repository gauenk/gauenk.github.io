About Me

 

In these exciting times, self-driving cars are taxis, robots are chefs, and large-language models assist coders, lawyers, and medical professionals alike. These exciting applications are possible because of amazing progress in large-scale deep neural networks. However, today's large networks methods have known limitations [1,2,3,4]. Training with GPUs is expensive and meaningfully contributes to climate change [4], our responsibility to secure data privacy restricts access to important data sources, the interpretability of deep features is lacking [2], and deep networks learn superficially [1]. Maybe the most pressing problem core to large-scale deep learning are unpredictable failures and untrustworthy results [1,2,3]. Large-scale deep neural networks are similar to the fictional creatures of Mistwraiths (or Shoggoth), which use bones of other creatures to construct their own skeleton. These creatures (aka large-scale networks) do not care if there are three skulls instead of one, or if the feet are where the hands should be. All that matters is that “they work” according to some loss function.

 

Inductive bias is one approach to solve these problems. It is the process of using human knowledge to improve the quality of a deep network on testing data. To extend our Mistwraith analogy, designing networks with good inductive biases “forces the Mistwraith to read”. In the graphic, this visually makes Mistwraith's more humanlike. Inductive bias is related to architecture design for deep neural networks, but extends beyond a specific benchmark or application. An excellent example of inductive bias is Geometric Deep Learning [5,6], which aims to improve representation learning by using known regularities of the physical world.

 

Self-similarity and superpixels act as the inductive bias in my research. My current focuses uses superpixels to improve the quality of networks modules for computer vision applications. I found superpixel attention is the optimal denoiser and significantly improves image denoising quality within a simple network. I have extended this idea other superpixel-based operators (such as superpixel convolution), which also shows promising results. I am actively studying the extension of space-only supeprixels to space-time superpixels, which I believe is important to improving our 3D understanding of a scene. For future work, I believe using latent 3D scene information will improve the quality of deep network modules for 2D images.

 

I love the idea of AI acting in the physical world, and I find it exciting we live in a world where self-driving cars exist. I would love to work on the algorithms for the feasible and safe commercialization of a home robot in the truest Jetson form. The kitchen robot is an example of one such project. These projects will take many decades of focused research across many disciplines, and I believe a core aspect of the solution will be specialized deep neural network modules using inductive bias.
